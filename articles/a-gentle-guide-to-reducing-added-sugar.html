🍯 A Gentle Guide to Reducing Added Sugar

◷ 6 minute read · Published April 2, 2024 · Educational health article

How to lower added sugar without shame, extreme restriction, or losing the foods you enjoy.

Reducing added sugar does not have to mean cutting out every dessert or policing every bite. Added sugar is common in sweet drinks, pastries, candy, flavored yogurts, cereals, sauces, and coffee drinks. The goal is to notice patterns and make swaps that still feel satisfying.

Start with beverages because they are one of the easiest places to adjust. Soda, sweet tea, energy drinks, and large flavored coffees can contain more sugar than people realize. Try a smaller size, half-sweet version, sparkling water, unsweetened tea, or coffee with less syrup. This one change can make a meaningful difference without changing every meal.

Pair sweet foods with meals rather than eating them alone when possible. Dessert after a balanced meal may feel more satisfying than candy on an empty stomach. Protein, fiber, and fat slow the experience down and reduce the urge to keep grazing.

Labels can help, but they should not become a source of anxiety. Compare similar products and choose the one with less added sugar when the taste still works for you. Plain yogurt with fruit and cinnamon, oatmeal with berries, or peanut butter on whole-grain toast can satisfy sweetness with more nutrients.

If you have diabetes, reactive hypoglycemia, a history of disordered eating, or a medical nutrition plan, get individualized advice. Food choices should support health without creating fear or guilt.

Key takeaways

  • Start with sweet drinks before changing everything.
  • Keep enjoyable foods in reasonable portions.
  • Use labels to compare similar products.
  • Avoid shame-based dieting language.
